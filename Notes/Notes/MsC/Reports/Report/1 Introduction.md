The tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) hosts complex pro-tumorigenic and continuously evolving environments that has been shown to play a pivotal role in cancer treatment, prevention and prognosis. TIME is a multifaceted system where progression is influenced by genetic/epigenetic changes and complex signaling networks where tumor cells work as the heart of the TIME to support their proliferation. Due to its compelling role, many efforts has been made to provide better understanding of the TIME to further enhance cancer treatment. The tumor space consists of tumor cells working adjacent to non-tumor microenvironments in frequent communication, which includes cell to cell contacts, free cell contacts and mediators enabling these contacts, secreted as molecules, factors and vesicles to ultimately enable transfer of genetic information. The tumor cells hijack non-cellular components and treatment strategies focus on obstructing this communication and neutralize tactics deployed by these cells to survive and resist anti-cancer modalities. The tumor landscape can vary greatly between patients and a demand for patient-level stratifying investigations has sparked development of new technologies to further tailor treatment in the immuno-oncology setting. [2,3] New technologies such as single-cell RNA sequencing pairs molecular information with molecular mechanisms driving tumor growth and has been used consistently in various studies further establishing the role of TIME in oncology research. [4,5,6] Flow cytometry have proved its utility in rapid diagnosis and classification of tumor cells and rapidly made advancements in hematology with its primary application involving identification of cell surface and intracellular proteins. [7] The combination of conventional immunofluorescence techniques with digital barcoded antibodies or oligonucleotides has established digital spatial profiling (DSP) as a novel method for high-plex spatial profiling of proteins and RNAs. This technology has been attractive to many investigators due to its potential to provide deep understanding of tumor immunology. [8]

With this new focus of developments, the dissection of TIME has provided opportunities to understand the tumorigenesis and evolution. However, their use in the clinical setting remains limited due to high cost, time consumption and complexity and limitations with the various solution approaches. DSP can interrogate a high number of biomarkers with high throughput, but lacks information at single-cell level [9], bulk RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) provides tissue-level summaries of transcriptional programs, but is limited when resolving single-cell heterogeneity [10], single-cell RNA sequencing suffer from high requirements on specimens and individual batch effects and limited sequencing depth [11], and mass flow cytometry requires fresh, viable samples and does not show cell morphology [7]. Additionally, standard spatial proteomics pipelines typically treat each marker independently and reduce multiplexed profiles to coarse categories, and this overlook affects cell states characterized by coordinated expression of multiple markers.
